### The Primitive Kinematic Equation


The baseline relationship between any two entities in a relational universe requires no background Cartesian grid. It is defined entirely by four native spatial metrics: a scalar separation distance ($r$), a relative radial velocity ($v_{\text{rad}}$), a tangential velocity ($v_{\text{tan}}$), and a rate of change of that velocity ($a$).

To maintain absolute relativity without absolute reference frames, stable relational motion must resolve as a localized rotational sweep around a mutual pivot point. The universal baseline equation governing all stable spatial boundaries is:

$$r = \frac{v\_\theta^2 + v\_r^2}{a}$$
Which aligns fully with the equation for centrepetal force: (ref?)
$$r = \frac{v^2}{a}$$

To calculate any event in the universe, we only need these four kinematic measurements. We do not need an independent definition for "time," because time is simply a measurement of distance divided by speed. If you know how far a fluid ripple travels ($r$) and how fast it moves ($v$), time is already completely accounted for.

#### The Four Kinematic Components (The Primitives)

The four dimensions are:

1. **Radius ($r$):** The physical size, boundary, or distance between two rotating points
2. **Tangential Velocity ($v_{\text{tan}}$):** The *spinning speed* around a central point.
3. **Radial Velocity ($v_{\text{rad}}$):** The speed of movement directly inward or outward from a central point.
4. **Acceleration ($a$):** The radial acceleration, and also the *restoring force* or stiffness of the field, which we shall see are equivalent.


##### The Relational Sign of Tangential Velocity (Intrinsic Spin)

The tangential velocity ($v_{\text{tan}}$) is defined not as a vector requiring an external coordinate grid, but as a signed relational scalar ($+v$ or $-v$). Because velocity is squared within the foundational primitive, the localized spatial footprint ($r$) remains perfectly invariant under a sign flip:

$$r = \frac{(+v)^2}{a} = \frac{(-v)^2}{a}$$

When two independent loops interface, their relative tangential velocity difference ($\Delta v_\theta$) collapses from complex vector trigonometry to a starkly simple algebraic subtraction:

$$\Delta v_\theta = v_{\theta, \text{system}} - v_{\theta, \text{incoming}}$$

##### Radial Velocity and state transition

By contrast, Radial Velocity is not an existential property, therefore it can be zero and it can and does transition between positive and negative. The positive / negative signing of the radial velocity does have important implications for the state of system however.

A negative radial velocity denotes a collapsing system, which continues to reduce until the system finds stability.

Conversely, a positive radial velocity creates an expanding system, leading to stabilisation at a larger radius, or infinite dissipation.

A zero radial velocity - or small fluctuations around zero - indicate a stable system and is **defined by the values of its other dimensions.**

##### State 1: The Expanding Wave 


We apply our primitive equation to the scenario of an expanding wave in a three dimensional field:

$$r = \frac{v\_\theta^2 + v\_r^2}{a}$$

* **$v\_r$ (Radial Velocity):** This is the forward propagation speed. It is the rate at which the wave shell is expanding _outward_ into unexcited space. This is bounded by the substrate's speed limit ($v\_r \approx c$).

* **$v\_\theta$ (Tangential Velocity):** This is the lateral extension vector. As a bubble expands, its surface area grows $v\_\theta$ is small, but it is never zero.

* **$r$ (The Radius):** This is the literal radius of the expanding wave bubble. It tracks exactly how far the wave front has traveled from its initial point of origin.

* **$a$ (The Localized Wave Acceleration):** This tracks the intensity (the pressure gradient) of the wave front.

Because the velocity is squared, as $r$ increases, and from our field equation $a$ decreases, therefore the velocity must increase exponentially, until it reaches it's maximum at the speed of light $c$.

##### State 2: Collision and Collapse

If we assume two of these spherical wave fronts, as they expand, their paths inevitably cross, and their outer footprints in their point fields begin to interact with each other.

Because their $v\_\theta$ components are parallel and, assuming they are phase-aligned (spinning in the same direction), they constructively interfere. At the initial zone of overlap, the local tangential velocity experiences a modest, incremental increase:

$$v\_{\theta, \text{local}} = v\_{\theta, A} + v\_{\theta, B}$$

As the two wave fronts increasingly interface, their cooperative alignment causes $v\_\theta$ to ramp up. Because the system's total velocity budget is constrained by the medium, the addition of tangential velocity, causes a reduction in radial velocity towards zero.

But it doesn't stop at zero. As they increasingly circle together, drawing toward a new shared center of mass. Relative to each other, **$v\_r$ becomes negative ($v\_r < 0$)**.

A negative radial velocity is the literal kinematic definition of an implosion or structural collapsing system. If we apply the primative equation, we see that the relationship between the radius and acceleration creates a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

Let's look at how a negative radial velocity behaves inside the primitive equation:

$$a = \frac{v\_\theta^2 + v\_r^2}{r}$$

Because the radial velocity is squared, $(-v\_r)^2$ remains a positive addition to the total kinetic energy numerator, the engine doesn't break down when a vector flips direction. However, because $v\_r = \frac{dr}{dt}$ is now negative, **the radius $r$ begins to rapidly shrink ($r \downarrow$)**.

Because $r$ is in the denominator, the moment it begins to shrink, **the acceleration containment gradient ($a$) is mathematically forced to shoot upward ($a \uparrow$)**.



##### Structural acceleration gradient spin and charge

Because both velocity components are squared in the numerator, the localized structural acceleration gradient ($a$) is the **direct cumulative sum of both the rotational mass-spin and the radial breathing-pulse**. A particle's total effect on the local vacuum stiffness is a compound profile. You cannot alter a particle's charge state without subtly shifting the baseline tension ($a$) of the substrate it is sitting in.


##### Velocity Saturation

As $a$ increase and $r$ reduces in a gravitational-like spin, the total velocity also increases until it begins to approach it's saturation limit towards the speed of light $c$. But the that linear closing velocity is continuously diverted into the orbital rotation ($v\_\theta$). As the orbit locks in and circularizes, the inward closing velocity drops toward zero ($v\_{\text{r}} \to 0$).
