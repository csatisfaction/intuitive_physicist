### 4.3.1 The Reciprocal Substrate Deformation Gradient

By running this continuous outward decay through the primitive equation, the spatial radius ($r$) of the deformation is shown to expand as the localized containment acceleration ($a$) drops off toward the environmental baseline:

$$r = \frac{v^2}{a\_{\text{decay}}}$$

This continuous, decaying spatial footprint is what classical physics records at long range as the **gravitational field**. Because geometric mass is a universal function of localized torque, this substrate deformation is an invariant property of **all** localized energy structures. Even a massless traveling photon ripple possesses a trace, non-zero geometric mass profile as it shears against the medium's saturation ceiling, meaning every propagating entity projects a surrounding field gradient into the substrate.

Consequently, every material system is defined by a distinct, two-tier spatial manifestation of a single acceleration gradient:

1. **The Wave-Core:** The hyper-dense, high-impedance standing wave cavity centered at the peak acceleration coordinate ($r\_{\text{core}} = c^2/a\_{\text{max}}$). This zone carries the bulk of the system’s localized torque and translates linearly through space as a bound vortex knot.

2. **The Substrate Deformation Gradient:** The broad, uncoiled, low-impedance field extension that propagates widely into the surrounding medium ahead of and around the core ($r \to \text{gargantuan}$), continuously mapping the geometric boundary conditions of local space.

Wave-particle duality is not a behavioral shift dictated by an observer; it is the physical, simultaneous manifestation of these two real, orthogonal components of a single structural footprint.

While individual impacts present the appearance of a random quantum lottery, this unpredictability is a consequence of sub-scale phase unresolvability.
